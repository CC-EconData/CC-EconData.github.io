This is significant because XQC played this game in 2020 allowing me to see if a streamer can increase the number of players a game has even after it has been released for several years. As demonstrated in figure 3, the month XQC played Turmoil – highlighted by yellow – received a massive boost in average players from the previous month. In figure 5, I regress average players on average viewers and do this for the months January 2019 to December 2021. The regression results in an R2 value of 0.68 and a coefficient of 5.27 statistically significant at a1% level. This is implies that a 1 unit increase in average viewership leads to a 5.27 unit increase in average players. Thus, this indicates a strong statistically significant relationship between the two variables. That is, the higher average viewers there are, the higher the number of average players there are.

What about during the month that games are released

Regression Link

The main difficulty of my project is the data I collected; that is, how representative Average Players is of the number of game sales. It could just be that, in the case of Turmoil for example, a number of players who originally bought the game when it first came out go back to it after a number of months due to a streamer they watch playing it. This would lead to the average player count being less representative of the number of sales a game has. In response to this, I decide to compare peak player count on Steam against total hours watched on Twitch for several games released during the month of their release. I do this as peak players should be more representative of sales here as it cannot be the case that peak players are increasing during release month because people are going back to the game; it must be due to people buying the game. Figure 6 demonstrates this regression. The regression results in an R2 value of 0.57 and a statistically significant coefficient at a 1% level.


Conclusion

To conclude, my findings imply that there is a statistically significant relationship between the games that are most watched on Twitch and those most played on Steam. While there are limitations to my data due to using a proxy for game sales, the fact that there is a significant relationship and similar R2 for both regression I do gives me confidence my results at least imply correlation even though it is not sufficient to indicate causality.
